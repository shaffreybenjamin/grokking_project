1. ONE-PARAGRAPH SUMMARY OF WHAT THE STATIC PHASE ESTABLISHED
================================================================================

A single-layer single-head transformer (Nanda/Poncini setup, d_model=128) and
a Chughtai-style one-hidden-layer MLP (d_hidden=256 after retraining; was 512)
were both trained to grok modular addition mod p=113 with matched
hyperparameters. The static (final-checkpoint) belief-geometry analysis
replicates Poncini (PIBBSS 2025) on the transformer cleanly — confirming that
the model linearly represents the sRRMod_p EHMM predictive vectors at all four
residual-stream positions and does NOT linearly represent the RRMod_p HMM
simplex vectors. The MLP shows a structurally different picture: it linearly
represents both the EHMM and the HMM simplex at the post-ReLU layer, not
because it has learned a different algorithm, but because its broader Fourier-
mode coverage makes the simplex linearly recoverable from its activations via
the discrete Dirichlet-kernel identity. Both architectures are implementing
the same algorithmic family (Fourier multiplication over Z/pZ); they differ
in *representational economy* — the transformer commits to a minimal Fourier
basis (4 dominant modes), the MLP uses a broader one (10+ similar-amplitude
modes), in keeping with Chughtai's weak universality.

2. THE BELIEF GEOMETRY CONCEPTUAL FRAMEWORK (FOR WRITEUP CHAPTER 2)
================================================================================

This is the abstract framework that grounds the empirical work. Four distinct
spaces are involved:

  Space                         What lives there                     Defined by

  Input space X                 token sequences (a, b, c ∈ Z/pZ)     the DGP
  Parameter space Θ ⊂ R^P       model weights (P ≈ 226k transf.)    architecture
  Activation space A_pos        intermediate activations a(x; θ)    arch + weights
                                in R^d_model at chosen layer pos.
  Belief / predictive-vector    comp-mech features of the process   the DGP only
    space V

The key conceptual move: V is determined entirely by the DGP, NOT by any model.
It's a structured submanifold of some ambient R^D (NOT a linear subspace —
this is a recurring confusion to head off in the writeup):

  - RRMod_p HMM:  V is the finite set of p one-hot vectors at simplex vertices
                  in R^p. "Simplex target" in our plots = elements of this set.
  - sRRMod_p EHMM: V is a torus = product of n circles, embedded in R^(2n).
                   "Polygon target" = points on this torus.

These are TWO PRESENTATIONS of the same DGP — they produce identical
conditional distributions P(future | history). In comp-mech terms they're
"predictively equivalent". The writeup should be careful never to say "the
HMM and the EHMM are two different DGPs"; they're two coordinate systems for
one DGP.

The central claim — the linear-representation hypothesis applied to NNs:

  > There exists a linear map W: R^d_model -> R^D such that
  > W · a(x; θ) ≈ ⟨v^(x)|  for all x ∈ X.

The R² of the corresponding regression measures how well this hypothesis
holds. R² ≈ 1: the model linearly encodes ⟨v^(x)| in its activations.
R² ≈ 0: the model's activations don't linearly align with this V.

The "linear" matters: activations are in R^d_model (e.g. 128 dims); V is
typically much lower-dimensional (2n = 8 dims for our transformer); a linear
projection compresses ~120 redundant dims away to land on V. The hypothesis
is that this projection is sufficient — no non-linear transformation needed.

3. CORE TRANSFORMER FINDING (FOR THE RESULTS CHAPTER)
================================================================================

3.1 The trained transformer is a clean replication of Poncini Section 5

    Per-position decoding R² (supervised, full d_model = 128 features):
      POS1 (post-embed @ a): R²(EHMM) = 1.000
      POS2 (post-embed @ b): R²(EHMM) = 1.000
      POS3 (post-attention): R²(EHMM) = 0.9998 (additive target ⟨v(a)|+⟨v(b)|)
      POS4 (post-MLP):       R²(EHMM) = 0.9930 (single-circle target ⟨v(c)|)
      POS4 simplex control:  R²(simplex) = 0.145

    The EHMM/HMM separation at POS4 is ~7x — the headline Poncini result.

    Logit-fit Fourier modes: ω = (49, 30, 25, 41) with large α². Four modes,
    not Poncini's five — both lie within the "3-7 modes" band typical of
    p=113 modular-addition runs with different seeds. This is Chughtai weak
    universality manifesting at the level of frequency count, not just choice.

3.2 The 6 "extra" frequencies in our max_freqs=10 fit are spurious harmonics

    With max_freqs=10 we get 6 additional frequencies (15, 10, 23, 53, 38, 34)
    with much smaller α². These are NOT additional Fourier modes the model is
    using — they are arithmetic byproducts of cos(ω₁)·cos(ω₂) products in the
    MLP. Concrete checks:

        49 + 30 = 79  →  113 − 79 = 34   ✓  (matches a fitted spurious mode)
        49 + 41 = 90  →  113 − 90 = 23   ✓
        49 + 25 = 74  →  113 − 74 = 39   ✗  (not in the list — not every
                                             arithmetic combination shows up)

    Diagnostic: per-frequency supervised R² for these spurious modes caps
    at ≈ 0.5. That's the signature of recovering only ONE of the two target
    dimensions — cos but not sin. The full cosine·cosine identity yields a
    sum/difference cosine but no sine, so the spurious modes appear in
    activations as cos-only signals, hence R² = 0.5 from linear regression
    onto a 2-D (cos, sin) target.

    Implication for writeup: when reporting "the model uses n frequencies",
    say n = 4 for our transformer and explain that fits with larger
    max_freqs pick up arithmetic harmonics that don't correspond to
    additional learned representations.

3.3 The cosine-only Poncini basis cannot reach R² = 1.0

    Poncini's logit form z(a,b,c) = Σ α_k² cos(2π ω_k (a+b−c)/p) uses ONLY
    cosines. Cosine is even in u = (a+b−c), so the basis can only fit
    functions of u that are symmetric under u → −u. Real trained models
    have small asymmetric (sine-of-u) residuals — adding more cosine
    frequencies does NOT close this gap. For our transformer:

        Logit-fit R² with  4 cosine modes ≈ 0.97
        Logit-fit R² with 10 cosine modes ≈ 0.98
        Logit-fit R² with 20 cosine modes ≈ 0.9878  (asymptote)

    This is NOT a fitting bug; it's a basis-coverage issue. The model is
    almost-but-not-quite symmetric in (a+b−c). For the writeup: if R²=0.99
    is presented as the success criterion, note that Poncini's specific
    model achieves it; ours asymptotes just below. Adding a sine basis
    would close the gap but diverges from Poncini's exact convention.

3.4 The "polygon width" at POS4 is real and Poncini-consistent

    Per-frequency PCA-fitted plots at POS4 show *thick rings* rather than
    infinitely-thin circles for the top 4 frequencies. This is Poncini's own
    observation: the MLP's modular-sum implementation doesn't produce an
    infinitely-sharp ⟨v(a+b)| — it produces an approximate version with
    non-trivial spread. This is interpretive content for the writeup: the
    polygon width is the model's "representational noise" at the post-modular-
    sum layer, and is visible because the MLP can only approximate the cosine-
    of-sum identity via its ReLU non-linearity.


================================================================================
4. CORE MLP FINDING (FOR THE RESULTS CHAPTER)
================================================================================

4.1 The MLP uses 10+ Fourier modes of similar amplitude

    Fitted ω = (22, 33, 41, 16, 51, 7, 36, 27, 19, 48) with α² spread roughly
    uniformly across them. Per-frequency supervised R²:

        At M1 (pre-ReLU):  R² = 1.000 for every fitted ω
        At M2 (post-ReLU): R² ≈ 0.85 for every fitted ω

    Every fitted frequency is a genuine learned mode — no spurious-harmonic
    pattern like the transformer. This is Chughtai weak universality at its
    most spread-out: same algorithmic family (Fourier multiplication), but
    distributed across many frequencies rather than concentrated on a few.

4.2 The MLP DOES linearly represent the simplex (and this is informative)

    At M2: R²(EHMM) ≈ 0.86 AND R²(simplex) ≈ 0.69.

    This is the empirical fact that most needs careful interpretation in the
    writeup. The naive reading — "the MLP has learned both DGPs" — is
    incorrect on two counts:
      (a) the EHMM and HMM-simplex are predictively-equivalent presentations
          of one DGP (see §2), so "learned both DGPs" is a category error;
      (b) high R²(simplex) at M2 doesn't indicate algorithm choice — it's a
          consequence of representation breadth.

    The correct explanation is the discrete Dirichlet-kernel identity:

        one_hot(c = c_0) = (1/p) Σ_{ω=0}^{p-1} cos(2π ω (c − c_0)/p)

    A one-hot at c_0 is exactly a Fourier sum over ALL p frequencies. A
    *partial* Fourier sum is a smoothed delta — narrower with more frequencies.
    So:

      - Transformer at POS4 represents ~4 frequencies → soft-delta projection
        onto the simplex is broad → linear regression to one-hot gives R²≈0.15
      - MLP at M2 represents 10+ frequencies → sharper soft-delta → linear
        regression to one-hot gives R²≈0.69

    Same algorithmic family (Fourier multiplication), same DGP, but DIFFERENT
    representational economy. The transformer is *compressive* (commits to a
    minimal basis); the MLP is *redundant* (uses a broader basis). The high
    simplex R² is a measure of Fourier-coverage breadth, NOT a measure of
    HMM-style algorithm choice.

    Writeup angle: this reframes Poncini's simplex R² as a "compression-
    sensitive diagnostic" rather than a clean DGP discriminator. Low simplex
    R² (transformer) → compressive Fourier basis. High simplex R² alongside
    high EHMM R² (MLP) → broad Fourier coverage. The asymmetry between the
    two architectures is therefore evidence about representational economy,
    not about which DGP each one chose.

4.3 The d_hidden=512 dilution problem and the retrain at d_hidden=256

    The original MLP was trained at d_hidden=512. With 10 frequencies × 2
    dims = 20-D Fourier subspace embedded in 512-D activation space, no
    single PC aligned with a single frequency's (cos, sin) plane — top-10
    PCs returned linear combinations across multiple frequencies, and
    per-frequency PCA-fitted plots returned junk for every row (the SAME
    junk pattern regardless of which ω the row claimed to be analysing).

    Diagnostic that this was a PCA-coverage issue, not a representation
    failure: per-frequency *supervised* R² at M1 was 1.000 for every
    fitted ω. The model represented the frequencies; the top 10 PCs
    just didn't isolate them.

    Two fixes in scope: (i) raise n_pcs to ~40 (enough to span the 20-D
    Fourier subspace); (ii) retrain at smaller d_hidden so the representation
    concentrates. The user did both — n_pcs=40 in the static plotting, plus
    a retrain at d_hidden=256 (matches Chughtai's reference setup). Trained
    for 100k epochs to ensure full grokking.

    Writeup angle: this is a useful methodological point about model
    selection for interpretability — smaller models force concentration
    of representations, which makes the inverse problem (reading the
    algorithm out of activations) more tractable. There's a real tension
    between "expressive enough to grok" and "concentrated enough to
    interpret" that the writeup should acknowledge.

4.4 Position-mismatch artifact (POS4 vs M2)

    POS4 (transformer) and M2 (MLP) are both immediately before unembed.
    But the transformer's POS4 is a residual stream — it still carries
    POS3's additive structure. The MLP's M2 has no residual — it IS the
    entire pre-unembed signal. So at M2 the activations are essentially
    W_U^+ · one_hot(c), which trivially supports both EHMM and simplex
    decoding. POS4 retains the additive structure cleanly.

    For a fair cross-architecture comparison, decode at the pre-MLP layer
    in each model:
        Transformer: POS3 (post-attention, pre-MLP-block)
        MLP:        M1   (pre-ReLU)

    At those layers, both architectures show R²(EHMM) climbing to ~1 and
    R²(simplex) staying near 0. This is the apples-to-apples comparison
    and it confirms both architectures form the same additive predictive
    structure ⟨v(a)|+⟨v(b)| before the modular-sum non-linearity.

6. METHODOLOGICAL SUBTLETIES TO WRITE UP CAREFULLY
================================================================================

6.1 The position-appropriate EHMM target
    POS1, POS2 → single-circle target indexed by the position's natural index
    POS3, M1   → additive ⟨v(a)|+⟨v(b)| (sum-of-two-circles, rose curve)
    POS4, M2   → single-circle ⟨v(a+b mod p)|
    Decoding at the wrong target gives R²=0 even when the model represents
    the right one (this was caught when an early version of the trajectory
    code tried v[c] at POS3 and produced uniformly-zero R²).

6.2 Ridge regression numerics
    Ridge alpha = 1e-3 for EHMM (20-D, well-conditioned).
    Ridge alpha = 1.0 for simplex (113 one-hot columns, ill-conditioned at
    untrained checkpoints — alpha=1e-3 produces negative R² spikes from the
    Cholesky solver running on rank-deficient activations).
    Both settings agree to ~0.005 at converged checkpoints, so no comparability
    cost.

6.3 Supervised vs semi-supervised decoding
    Supervised  = full activations → target via ridge.  Tests whether the
                  representation IS THERE.
    Semi-supervised = PCA(n_pcs) → ridge → target.  Tests whether the
                  representation is in the top-K PCs (i.e. is dominant).
    For sparse-frequency models (transformer): top-K PCs concentrate on
    learned frequencies, so n_pcs ≈ 2 · n_freq suffices.
    For spread-frequency models (MLP): PCs are linear combinations of many
    frequencies and don't isolate individual ones; n_pcs needs to span the
    full Fourier subspace.

6.4 Per-frequency R² ≈ 0.5 = cos-only signal
    The signature "supervised R² ≈ 0.5 against a 2-D (cos, sin) target"
    means the regression recovers EITHER cos OR sin but not both.
    Causes: (a) the frequency is a cosine·cosine harmonic byproduct
    (transformer §3.2); (b) the model represents only a partial phase of
    the frequency. Worth flagging this signature in the writeup as a
    distinguishable footprint.

6.5 Logit gauge fixing
    Softmax is invariant under per-(a,b) additive shifts in logits. The
    fit_frequencies routine mean-centres logits per (a,b) before fitting.
    This makes the cumulative R² scale-invariant and gauge-invariant.

7. INTERPRETIVE CAUTIONS — THINGS THE WRITEUP COULD EASILY MISSTATE
================================================================================

7.1 "The transformer learned the EHMM and not the HMM" — NO.
    Both EHMM and HMM are presentations of the same DGP. The transformer
    *represents the process* using a polygon-style coordinate system; it
    doesn't choose between two competing DGPs. Phrase as "the transformer's
    activations are linearly aligned with the EHMM presentation but not
    with the HMM-simplex presentation" or "the model commits to a Fourier-
    basis representation, not a one-hot-basis representation".

7.2 "The MLP learned both DGPs" — NO.
    The MLP represents the same single DGP using a broader Fourier basis;
    the simplex linear-recoverability is a Dirichlet-kernel side effect of
    that broad coverage (see §4.2). Phrase as "the MLP's broader Fourier
    coverage makes both presentations linearly accessible from its
    activations, indicating low representational economy rather than
    distinct algorithm choice".

7.3 "Belief geometry is a subspace of R^D" — NO.
    It's a structured submanifold (or finite point set) in ambient R^D, not
    a linear subspace. For RRMod_p it's a finite set of simplex vertices;
    for sRRMod_p it's a torus. The linear-representation hypothesis is that
    a linear projection of activations lands ON this manifold, not that
    activations span a copy of it.

7.4 "Belief geometry analysis requires knowing the DGP" — STRICTLY YES,
    BUT...
    The canonical predictive vector ⟨v^(w)| is defined in terms of the
    process's transition matrices, so without the DGP you cannot write
    down V explicitly. But:
      (a) CSSR-style empirical causal-state reconstruction recovers an
          empirical V from data alone.
      (b) Candidate-DGP comparison: posit a family of candidates, derive
          each one's V, pick the best linear fit.
      (c) The methodology (regression, polygon-width diagnostics, per-
          frequency analysis) is process-agnostic and transfers.
    The toy-case work is therefore a tool-building exercise that grounds
    the bootstrap to unknown-DGP cases — see §8.

7.5 "More frequencies = more complex algorithm" — NOT NECESSARILY.
    The transformer uses 4 frequencies and groks; the MLP uses 10+ and
    grokks. The MLP isn't doing a more complex algorithm — it's doing the
    same algorithm with more representational redundancy. n_dominant (the
    threshold-free count of "frequencies with α² > 10% of max α²") is the
    better complexity measure than raw frequency count.

8. THE BOOTSTRAP PROGRAMME (FOR THE DISCUSSION / FUTURE-WORK CHAPTER)
================================================================================

The thesis-level research direction: use belief-geometry tools to *reverse-
engineer the DGP* from a trained model's activations on data alone, building
on the toy-case validation that's been done.

8.1 Why this is well-posed
    Toy cases provide ground truth: we know the DGP, so "did the bootstrap
    recover it?" is a falsifiable question. Plus, trained models are
    learned compressions of conditional distributions, so model activations
    are a denoised summary of empirical statistics — using model + data
    jointly is more identifiable than data-only causal-state reconstruction.

8.2 What the toy-case work has built
    A calibrated toolkit:
      - regression-based decoding pipeline (supervised + semi-supervised)
      - per-frequency analysis with PCA→ridge
      - polygon-width / harmonic / spurious-mode diagnostics
      - position-by-position decomposition (where in the network the
        representation lives)
      - developmental signatures (n_dominant, α-entropy, etc.)
    Plus an interpretive vocabulary: "compressive vs redundant Fourier
    coverage", "additive vs post-modular-sum positions", "polygon width
    as representational noise".

8.3 The natural three-paper structure
    Paper 1 (WHAT):  Catalogue geometric signatures across a small library
                     of known DGPs (cyclic, non-abelian, mixtures, factored).
                     Establish robustness to architecture, seed,
                     hyperparameters.
    Paper 2 (INVERSE): Given activations + data only, identify which DGP
                     family best explains the model. Validate on held-out
                     toy cases.
    Paper 3 (EXTEND): Apply to cases where the catalogue doesn't already
                     contain the right DGP. Demonstrate identification of
                     structural features (symmetry group, factor count,
                     ergodicity) without naming the specific process.

8.4 Hard parts to be honest about
    (a) Predictive equivalence: multiple DGPs can give identical
        conditional distributions; the "right" target is the equivalence
        class, not a specific presentation.
    (b) Approximate processes: natural data isn't a GHMM; the bootstrap
        will need to handle "this model represents *something approximately
        like* an EHMM with these symmetries" rather than producing a clean
        process specification.
    (c) Weak universality: same DGP + same architecture, different seeds,
        give different specific Fourier-basis representations. The bootstrap
        must read out the FAMILY (EHMM-on-cyclic-group) without depending
        on the specific parameterisation.
    (d) Natural-language LLMs are not in initial scope — graduate up a
        complexity ladder: cyclic groups → non-abelian (S_n) → mixtures →
        factored / hierarchical → eventually language. Each rung is its
        own contribution.

8.5 Connection to alignment / interpretability
    If you can identify what statistical structure a model is exploiting,
    you can ask whether it's the structure you intended. This is the
    interpretability-and-safety angle. Belief geometry as a model-agnostic
    algorithm-identification tool is the contribution.

9. CONNECTIONS TO LITERATURE
================================================================================

  Poncini (2025 PIBBSS report)
      Primary thing being replicated and extended. Section 5 is the static-
      phase analysis target. We replicate the transformer findings; we extend
      to the MLP and show the position-matters / Dirichlet-kernel story.

  Chughtai et al. (2023) — "A toy model of universality"
      Weak universality: same algorithmic family across runs/architectures
      but specific representations differ. Both the transformer's frequency
      count (4 vs Poncini's 5) and the MLP's spread coverage (10+ vs the
      transformer's 4) instantiate this finding.

  Shai et al. — belief state geometry in transformers
      The original linear-representation-of-predictive-vectors finding.
      Mess3 process is their canonical example. Our cyclic-group setup is
      the modular-addition analogue.

  Piotrowski et al. — constrained belief updates
      The mechanism by which the attention layer constructs belief features
      from raw embeddings. Relevant for explaining WHY POS3 has the additive
      structure and POS4 the modular-sum structure.

  Stander et al. (2024) / Wu et al. (2024) — coset circuits / ρ-set circuits
      Alternative interpretations of group-multiplication networks. The
      belief-geometry approach is complementary: it reads the algorithm out
      of activations geometrically rather than from weight inspection, and
      so can in principle adjudicate between weight-level interpretations.

  Nanda et al. (2023) — progress measures for grokking
      Fourier-mode analysis of grokking transformers. Our logit-fit
      machinery is the comp-mech-style formalisation of his Fourier
      progress measure.

  Crutchfield — CSSR / causal-state reconstruction
      The empirical-V construction from data alone (no DGP needed). The
      bootstrap programme of §8 builds on this.

  Kumar et al. (2024) — lazy-to-rich grokking
      Frames grokking as a phase transition. Belief geometry should track
      this transition (developmental phase, see developmental_handoff.txt).
